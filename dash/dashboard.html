Methods Descriptions

Descriptions
  Convergence-Entropy

Convergence-Entropy (CE: Rosen & Dale 2024) is a measurement of how much the information in one utterance $x$ could be recovered from the content in a separate utterance $y$. It uses a large language model to generate representations of each token and it's contextual meaning and iterates over those representations to calculate the Shannon entropy for each token in some utterance $x$.

$\begin{aligned} E_{xi} &= LLM(i \in x) \\ P(E_{xi}|E_y) &= P_{N} \left( \min_j (E_{xi}, E_{yj}) | \mu=0, \sigma \right) \\ H[x|y] &= -\sum_i P(E_{xi}|E_y) \log P(E_{xi}|E_y) \end{aligned}$

Time-series and plots are plots are of convergence entropy for a prior utterance $x$ during conversation, and subsequent utterances $y$ that occur $t$ turns after the utterance $x$.

  Allan Variance

Allan Variance is a measurement of variation over time in a signal or statistical distribution (Allan & Levine 2016; Snyder 1981) and is measured as the total difference in a measurement for a time-series, minus it's future value and the how much that future value varies from the subsequent measurement. Formulaically, it is calculated as follows:

$AVAR = \frac{1}{2\tau^2} \Big\langle \left( x_{t+2n} - 2x_{t+n} - x_t \right)^2 \Big\rangle$

Notably, the log-log transformed Allan Variance and temporal measurement for a given signal can be used to calculate exponential growth in a signal, $\alpha$ (Abney et al 2014) -- which is used to characterize shifts in shared semantic and conceptual structures during conversation in the rest of this report.

  Corpora

Several corpora were used in the course of this study--their abbreviations/acronyms are available in the various sections of this visual report.

Monolingual English Conversations

Summary and report

Concepts shared between utterances have a durative, though temporally limited effect. What concepts are expressed by any one speaker appear to more strongly influence/are easier to recover from the same speaker's subsequent utterances, though utterances clearly influence the concepts expressed by both the "self" and "others" during interaction. The change in Entropy (see the top figure below, and the upper figure for each individual corpus) is exponential. Notably, as time progresses the difference in how much an utterance $x$ influences a speaker's own contributions to conversation and their partners' shrinks exponentially as well (see the lower figure below).

Exponential growth parameters for $\alpha$ are as follows

  • self: ($\alpha=-.199, SE=.001, t(519389)=-144.27, p < 1e^{-9}$)
  • other: ($\alpha=-.141, SE=.001, t(509862)=-79.32, p< 1e^{-9}$)
  • other-self: ($\alpha=-.178, SE=.002, t(496276)=-114.87, p< 1e^{-9}$)

CANDOR Exploratory Study

Summary and report

Results across 1656 of the conversations in the CANDOR dataset indicate that how one converges may differentially affect either prosocial affinity or the perception of establishing shared context.

People do not look for similarity, they look for synergy--the ability to jointly move from one set of concepts to the next with their partner as a marker of conversational quality.

Multilingual Conversations Across Languages

Summary and report

Analyses of 8 additional languages, plus a subset of the English conversation data, replicates the same temporal and interpersonal characteristics of the English monolingual study.

Exponential growth parameters for $\alpha$ are as follows

  • self: ($\alpha=-.111, SE=.001, t(347984)=-74.79, p< 1e^{-9}$)
  • other: ($\alpha=-.060, SE=.002, t(285890)=-34.45, p< 1e^{-9}$)
  • other-self: ($\alpha=-.103, SE=.00189, t(2833643)=-54.67, p<`e^{-9}$)
