ABOUT ME

My name is Zachary Rosen. I'm a Data Scientist and human behavior/modeling specialist. While I invite you to take a look at my CV for more information about the roles I've taken on over the years, the thread that I think ties my prior work together is fairly simple: I'm interested in understanding human behavior first and foremost. I think from there, everything else--including building out machine learning algorithms or deploying solutions from companies like openAI--naturally falls into place.

Start by understanding people, and you'll accomplish your goals!

My main area of interest is in leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) and LLM generated Embeddings to understand social networks and consumer behavior. In addition to that, my NLU work tends to rely on a mixture of neural network methods, Bayesian Statistics, and Monte Carlo methods.

As a data scientist and computational linguist I'm interested in quite a lot of things. But chief among those is that humans are weird, we do bizarre things with language, and those bizarre things are both really beautiful to document and extremely valuable for generating business insights.

RESEARCH & PROFESSIONAL INTERESTS

User Behavior Modeling:
One of the unifying themes of my work is understanding social relationships through data. I apply a variety of methodologies to this task. This area of inquiry, when combined with NLP methods, is particularly useful in tracking the spread of policy and/or marketing messages amongst individuals exposed to it.

Language Modeling/LLMs:
I group recent advances in LLMs in this category considering that they rely on the same underlying principles as prior language models (transformer models of lexical semantics). Language modeling is a powerful way of representing the meaning of people's utterances online and in person. My work relies primarily on transformer language models like GPT-* and BLOOM, and earlier transformer models like BERT to capture the variety of ways people express their wants, needs, thoughts, and ideas. The most interesting use of these techniques is in social-linguistic networking--building graphs of how people are connected to one another based on language patterns. This latter point can be used in a variety of ways, ranging from tracking hate speech online to creating marketing segments.

Bayesian Statistical Modeling:
As a set of techniques, Bayesian Statistical Modeling (BSM) is an invaluable statistical tool. For example, some prior work I've done leveraged Latent Mixture Modeling and Monte Carlo sampling to uncover unspoken customer motiviations for purchasing products based on the attributes of those products and customer purchasing history.

Deep Learning:
Deep learning continues to be a valuable tool in any data scientist's toolkit. For example, it is costly to fine-tune LLMs. However, some of my prior work at Wizard showed that you could use LLM generated embeddings and pass those to an LSTM to create a much more efficient classifier with significantly less required computational overhead.
