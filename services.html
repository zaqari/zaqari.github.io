Generative Exploratory Data Intelligence (GEDI)

There's a lot about contemporary approaches to Exploratory Data Analysis that leave me feeling uncomfortable.

Off the shelf tools for making sense of unstructured data tend to be poor characterizations of the actual distribution in that data. If you're dealing with text, a transformer based topic model (based on clustering and TFIDF) is likely showing you the results of that clustering process in a way that reporting on it directly to a client is liable to lead them astray. Extracting meaning from your work requires a computational linguist's level of understanding, and most stakeholders don't take the time to listen to pracitioners like you to hash out what is actually happening in the reporting you present.

You could use an LLM to systematically summarize the data. But there-in lies a new set of problems: between the window size which precludes you from looking at truly large datasets, the cost for running individual sentences, and the risk (or rather, the gaurantee) of outright "hallucinated" results that thus requires you to manually analyze the data anyways, the risks far outweigh the rewards.

Generative Exploratory Data Intelligence (GEDI: pronounced like the way a Star Wars geek would pronounce it) is an outright paradigm inversal of the way you would typically use an LLM to explore data. If you're interested in that kind of analysis, let's talk about sharing the templates and codebase required to do that.

Statistical Modeling/Consulting

Sometimes you can't just run a regression model and get the insights you need.

A lot of statistical methods for extracting insights from data are rooted in a lot of assumptions that might hurt their usability when you report on them. Regression, for example, is highly interpretable when compared to other deep learning methods, but it's inappropriate for time series forecasting or making sense of stochastic processes without modification (hence the need for Granger regression and other techniques). And even then, sometimes you might need something a little more tailored in order to answer the questions you have about people from their data if you need a direct answer to that question.

Understanding the link between model formalisms and the hypotheses they test is my jam. I'd love to help you flesh out your specific questions, pick or design a statistical model to answer those questions, and get you the code required to do that.
