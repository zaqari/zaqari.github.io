How People Dynamically Create Common Ground During Interactions
LLMs, Information Theory,

What do you get when you mix information theory with a deep interest in how people create common ground?

Work in areas like Communication Accommodation Theory (CAT), Conceptual Pact Theory (CPT), and Interactive Alignment Theory (IAT) posit that in order for conversation to happen at all, individuals have to align on how they discuss and understand their environment. And what ideas and concepts they converge on together forms the basis of their common ground.

My work in this area makes use of language technologies like Large Language Models, and statistical frameworks like information theory, to better understand what makes a conversation work. And, potentially, guide folks with novel insights into how to have better, more meaningful conversations in a data driven way.

The effects of Hate Speech on Measures of Social Language Use
LLMs, Information Theory

Research into hate speech tends to focus on one of two things: (1) How hate speech affects the groups and identities targeted by it, and (2) the psychological characteristics of people who use hate speech.

What's missing from these conversations though is twofold: (1) an understanding of how hate speech fundamentally shifts the discourse around it, and (2) how social feedback influences subsequent hateful behavior. Myself and collaborators at UCLA and UCSB are working to unpack the ways hate speech changes the flow and exchange of information in web-based discourse in an effort to better tackle these outstanding questions. We may not be able to eliminate hateful rhetoric in online spaces, but knowing how hate speech works on both of these axes can empower us to push back on hate with greater persuasive force.

Differences in LLM and Human Discourse Dynamics
LLMs, Information Theory, Pedagogy

Despite being lauded as open-domain conversational agents, LLMs exhibit strong differences from humans when it comes to engaging in prolonged discourse. Some of my recent work shows that these differences may be due to LLMs being incapable of replicating human motivations for introducing novelty into ongoing conversations.

In addition to these findings, I work on formal proofs showing that tuning LLM behavior to accomplish typical NLP tasks--like intent classification and topic modeling--might actually be largely intractable. This line of work focuses on defining the information theoretic requirements of such tasks, and the degree to which we can be confident that unstructured LLM responses can meet those requirements.

Socio-Semantic Networks with Large Language Models
LLMs, Graph-ML Methods, Monte Carlo Sampling

One of the things that is particularly interesting to me as a student of human behavior is the degree to which humans seem to just naturally form networked communities, using nothing but language and a little pro-social panache. Using convergence analysis to induce edge weights between individuals based on text data, this work requires the use of embeddings generated by large language models, information theoretic models of communication, and monte carlo sampling for testing and evaluation. I am working with collaborators right now on using this for bibliometric analyses of researcher outputs.
